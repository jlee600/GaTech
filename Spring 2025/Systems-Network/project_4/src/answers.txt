800 ms timeslice yields 131 context switches, 67.9 s execution, and 317.1 s in READY; at 600 ms you see 155 context switches, 68.0 s execution, and 303.0 s READY;

-> These findings indicate that as timeslice becomes smaller, the number of context switches increases extremely high—high rate of preemption—while execution time overall becomes almost independent of timeslice size; 
overall duration processes remain in the ready state becomes less as small timeslices cause high-speed cycling on the ready queue. Nonetheless, in a typical operating system, very brief timeslices are not usually good 
because the overhead due to high context switching (e.g., cache invalidation and higher scheduler overhead) may have a material impact on total system performance even if it lowers wait time.

Problem-3 (Preemptive Priority)
--------------------------------
/* Fix me */
To avoid starvation in preemptive priority scheduling, operating systems generally employ priority aging. This method increases the effective priority of a process the longer it has been in the ready queue. 
That is, every process has a base priority, but as time passes, its functional priority gets better (numerically lower if lower numbers are higher priority), so even low-priority processes will be scheduled sooner or later. 
This avoids indefinite postponement. 

In this project, we employ the formula: functional_priority = base_priority - (current_time - enqueue_time) * age_weight
This achieves equity without losing the responsiveness of more-priority tasks.

Problem-4 (The Priority Inversion Problem)
-------------------------------------------
/* Fix me */

To remove priority inversion in a non-preemptive priority scheduler, a policy such as priority inheritance can be implemented. 
When a low-priority process (such as the window manager) is holding up a high-priority process (such as P1), 
then the scheduler will temporarily raise the priority of the waiting low-priority process to that of the dependent high-priority process. 
This prevents the interference from medium-priority processes and enables the dependent high-priority process to execute as soon as possible.

We would do this in our simulator by tracking resource dependencies. If a high-priority process is waiting for a lower-priority process to finish, 
the lower-priority process is "boosted" in the queue to run before any process of medium priority. When it has finished its work (i.e., processed the window), 
its priority drops back to what it originally was. 
This enforces respecting of dependencies and does not let high-priority processes be starved indirectly by lower-priority processes being preempted.
